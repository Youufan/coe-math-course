Section 1 of 12

Why Complex Numbers?

Chapter 1 extends the number line into a plane so equations like \(x^2+1=0\) finally have roots.

Number ladder\(\mathbb N\subseteq\mathbb Z\subseteq\mathbb Q\subseteq\mathbb R\subseteq\mathbb C\). Real numbers fill the number line. Complex numbers fill a plane.
The problem
\(x^2+1=0\Rightarrow x^2=-1\)
There is no real \(x\) with \(x^2=-1\), because \(x^2\ge0\) for all real \(x\).
The new symbol
\(i^2=-1\)
So \(x^2+1=0\) has roots \(x=i\) and \(x=-i\).

Powers of \(i\)

Remainder trick\(i^{4k}=1,\quad i^{4k+1}=i,\quad i^{4k+2}=-1,\quad i^{4k+3}=-i\). Use the remainder after dividing the exponent by \(4\).
TRY Powers of \(i\)
Evaluate \(i^{179}+i^{2017}\).
Find remainders mod \(4\): \(179=4(44)+3\), so \(i^{179}=i^3=-i\).\(2017=4(504)+1\), so \(i^{2017}=i\).\(-i+i=0\).
Section 2 of 12

Rectangular Form & Equality

A complex number is two real numbers packaged together: horizontal part plus vertical part.

DefinitionA complex number has the form \(z=x+yi\), where \(x,y\in\mathbb R\). Real part: \(\operatorname{Re}(z)=x\). Imaginary part: \(\operatorname{Im}(z)=y\). The set of all complex numbers is \(\mathbb C\).
NumberReal partImaginary part
\(3+5i\)\(3\)\(5\)
\(3.5-i\)\(3.5\)\(-1\)
\(-\sqrt3+i\)\(-\sqrt3\)\(1\)
\(\pi+9i\)\(\pi\)\(9\)
Real numbers inside \(\mathbb C\)
Every real number \(x\) is also \(x+0i\). So \(\mathbb R\subseteq\mathbb C\).
Purely imaginary
\(z=x+yi\) is purely imaginary when \(x=0\), e.g. \(-\sqrt7\,i\).

Equality: match both parts

Rule\(x+yi=x'+y'i\) iff \(x=x'\) and \(y=y'\).
EXAMPLE 5 Match real and imaginary parts
Suppose \(x,y\in\mathbb R\) and \((2x-3)+5i=(x+7)-(y+1)i\). Find \(x\) and \(y\).
Real parts: \(2x-3=x+7\Rightarrow x=10\).
Imaginary parts: \(5=-(y+1)\Rightarrow y+1=-5\Rightarrow y=-6\).
\(x=10,\ y=-6\).
TRY Quick equality
If \((a+2)+bi=7+(3a-1)i\), find \(a,b\).
Real parts: \(a+2=7\Rightarrow a=5\).Imaginary parts: \(b=3a-1=15-1=14\).\(a=5,\ b=14\).

Argand Diagram

The real part is horizontal. The imaginary part is vertical. A complex number becomes a point or vector.

\(3-3i\)
\(3-3i=3\sqrt2\,\mathrm{cis}(-\pi/4)\)
Visual read\(z=x+yi\) is the point \((x,y)\). The same point can be viewed as a vector from \((0,0)\) to \((x,y)\).
Section 4 of 12

Modulus, Argument & Polar Form

Rectangular form says where the point is. Polar form says how far and at what angle.

Core formulasFor \(z=x+yi\): \(|z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\), and an argument \(\theta\) satisfies \(x=|z|\cos\theta,\ y=|z|\sin\theta\). Principal argument: \(-\pi\lt\theta\le\pi\).
Polar and exponential forms
\(z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)=r\,\mathrm{cis}\,\theta=re^{i\theta}\)
EXAMPLE 7 From rectangular to polar
Let \(z=3-3i\). Find \(|z|\), the principal argument, polar form, and exponential form.
\(|z|=\sqrt{3^2+(-3)^2}=\sqrt{18}=3\sqrt2\).
The point \((3,-3)\) is in quadrant IV, with reference angle \(\pi/4\).
Principal argument: \(\theta=-\pi/4\).
Polar: \(z=3\sqrt2\left(\cos(-\pi/4)+i\sin(-\pi/4)\right)\).Exponential: \(z=3\sqrt2\,e^{-i\pi/4}\).
EXAMPLE 8 From exponential to rectangular
Express \(z=5e^{-\frac{5\pi}{3}i}\) in rectangular form.
Use \(e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta\).\(-\frac{5\pi}{3}\) is coterminal with \(\frac{\pi}{3}\).\(z=5\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}\right)=5\left(\frac12+i\frac{\sqrt3}{2}\right)\).\(z=\frac52+\frac{5\sqrt3}{2}i\).
TRY Four quadrants
Find \(|z|\) and principal \(\arg z\) for \(z=-1-\sqrt3\,i\).
\(|z|=\sqrt{(-1)^2+(-\sqrt3)^2}=2\).The point is in quadrant III. Reference angle is \(\pi/3\).Principal argument must be in \((-\pi,\pi]\), so \(\arg z=-\frac{2\pi}{3}\).Polar form: \(2\,\mathrm{cis}\left(-\frac{2\pi}{3}\right)\).
Section 5 of 12

Conjugates

Conjugation reflects a complex number in the real axis. It flips the sign of the imaginary part.

DefinitionIf \(z=x+yi\), then \(\bar z=x-yi\). In exponential form, if \(z=re^{i\theta}\), then \(\bar z=re^{-i\theta}\).
\(z\)\(\bar z\)
\(3+5i\)\(3-5i\)
\(3.5-i\)\(3.5+i\)
\(-\sqrt3+i\)\(-\sqrt3-i\)
\(\pi+9i\)\(\pi-9i\)
\(-\sqrt7\,i\)\(\sqrt7\,i\)
Useful facts\(\bar{\bar z}=z\). \(z\) is real iff \(z=\bar z\). \(z\) is purely imaginary iff \(z=-\bar z\). Also \(z\bar z=|z|^2\).
TRY Conjugate in polar form
If \(z=2\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}\right)\), write \(\bar z\) in polar and rectangular form.
Polar conjugate: \(\bar z=2\left(\cos(-\frac{\pi}{3})+i\sin(-\frac{\pi}{3})\right)\).Rectangular: \(z=1+\sqrt3 i\), so \(\bar z=1-\sqrt3 i\).
Section 6 of 12

Addition, Subtraction & Multiplication

Addition and subtraction work by matching components. Multiplication expands normally, then replaces \(i^2\) with \(-1\).

Add/subtractIf \(z_1=x_1+y_1i\), \(z_2=x_2+y_2i\), then \(z_1\pm z_2=(x_1\pm x_2)+(y_1\pm y_2)i\).
Example 13(a)
\((3+5i)+(3.5-i)=6.5+4i\)
Example 13(b)
\((-\sqrt3+i)-(\pi+9i)=(-\sqrt3-\pi)-8i\)

Multiplication

\((x_1+y_1i)(x_2+y_2i)=(x_1x_2-y_1y_2)+(x_1y_2+x_2y_1)i\)
EXAMPLE 15 Multiply
Evaluate \((3+5i)(2-i)\).
Expand: \((3+5i)(2-i)=6-3i+10i-5i^2\).
Since \(i^2=-1\), \(-5i^2=5\).
Collect: \(6+5+7i\).
\((3+5i)(2-i)=11+7i\).
Polar meaningIf \(z_1=r_1\mathrm{cis}\,\theta_1\), \(z_2=r_2\mathrm{cis}\,\theta_2\), then \(z_1z_2=r_1r_2\,\mathrm{cis}(\theta_1+\theta_2)\). Multiply lengths, add angles.
TRY Multiplication as rotation
Let \(z=2\,\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{6}\). What does multiplying by \(\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{2}\) do to \(z\)?
The modulus stays \(2\), because \(|\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{2}|=1\).The argument increases by \(\frac{\pi}{2}\): \(\frac{\pi}{6}+\frac{\pi}{2}=\frac{2\pi}{3}\).Result: \(2\,\mathrm{cis}\frac{2\pi}{3}\). Geometrically: rotate \(z\) anticlockwise by \(90^\circ\).
Section 7 of 12

Division

Division uses the conjugate to make the denominator real. In polar form, division subtracts angles.

Conjugate trickFor nonzero \(z_2\), \(\displaystyle \frac{z_1}{z_2}=\frac{z_1\bar z_2}{z_2\bar z_2}=\frac{z_1\bar z_2}{|z_2|^2}\).
EXAMPLE 18 Divide by a complex number
Evaluate \(\displaystyle \frac{3+5i}{2-i}\).
Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate \(2+i\).
\(\displaystyle \frac{3+5i}{2-i}\cdot\frac{2+i}{2+i}=\frac{(3+5i)(2+i)}{(2-i)(2+i)}\).
Top: \(6+3i+10i+5i^2=1+13i\).
Bottom: \(4-i^2=5\).
\(\displaystyle \frac{3+5i}{2-i}=\frac15+\frac{13}{5}i\).
Polar divisionIf \(z_1=r_1\mathrm{cis}\theta_1\), \(z_2=r_2\mathrm{cis}\theta_2\), then \(\displaystyle \frac{z_1}{z_2}=\frac{r_1}{r_2}\mathrm{cis}(\theta_1-\theta_2)\).
EXAMPLE 19 Reciprocal of a unit complex number
Let \(z=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta\). Find \(|z|\) and show \(\frac1z=\bar z\).
\(|z|=\sqrt{\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta}=1\).\(\displaystyle \frac1z=\frac{\bar z}{z\bar z}=\frac{\cos\theta-i\sin\theta}{1}\).\(\displaystyle \frac1z=\cos\theta-i\sin\theta=\bar z\).
Section 8 of 12

Quadratics & Conjugate Roots

Over \(\mathbb C\), every polynomial has enough roots. Polynomials with real coefficients pair complex roots with their conjugates.

Quadratic formula in \(\mathbb C\)For \(ax^2+bx+c=0\), \(D=b^2-4ac\). If \(D\lt0\), then \(\displaystyle x=\frac{-b\pm i\sqrt{-D}}{2a}\).
EXAMPLE 21 Irreducible quadratic
Solve \(2x^2-3x+5=0\).
\(D=(-3)^2-4(2)(5)=9-40=-31\).
\(\displaystyle x=\frac{-(-3)\pm i\sqrt{31}}{2(2)}\).
\(\displaystyle x=\frac{3\pm i\sqrt{31}}{4}\).
Fundamental theorem of algebraA degree \(n\) polynomial with complex coefficients has exactly \(n\) complex roots, counting multiplicity.
Real coefficientsIf \(p(x)\) has real coefficients and \(z\) is a root, then \(\bar z\) is also a root. So complex roots with nonzero imaginary part arrive in conjugate pairs.
TRY Use the conjugate root
Given \(1+i\) is a root of \(x^3-x^2+2=0\), find all roots.
Since coefficients are real, \(1-i\) is also a root.Quadratic factor: \((x-(1+i))(x-(1-i))=((x-1)-i)((x-1)+i)=(x-1)^2+1=x^2-2x+2\).Divide \(x^3-x^2+2\) by \(x^2-2x+2\), giving \(x+1\).Roots: \(1+i,\ 1-i,\ -1\).
Section 9 of 12

De Moivre's Theorem

De Moivre turns powers of complex numbers into angle multiplication.

TheoremFor every integer \(n\), \((\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n=\cos(n\theta)+i\sin(n\theta)\).
EXAMPLE 25 Read the power
Express in the form \(\cos(n\theta)+i\sin(n\theta)\): (a) \((\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^9\), (b) \((\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^{-4}\).
(a) \(\cos9\theta+i\sin9\theta\).(b) \(\cos(-4\theta)+i\sin(-4\theta)\), also \(\cos4\theta-i\sin4\theta\).
EXAMPLE 26 Simplify powers
Simplify: (a) \(\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{4}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{4}\right)^{-2}\), (b) \(\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}\right)^9\).
(a) Angle becomes \(-2\cdot\frac{\pi}{4}=-\frac{\pi}{2}\). Result: \(\cos(-\frac{\pi}{2})+i\sin(-\frac{\pi}{2})=\)\(-i\).(b) Angle becomes \(9\cdot\frac{\pi}{3}=3\pi\). Result: \(\cos3\pi+i\sin3\pi=\)\(-1\).
TRY Reverse De Moivre
Write \(\cos5\theta-i\sin5\theta\) in the form \((\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n\).
\(\cos5\theta-i\sin5\theta=\cos(-5\theta)+i\sin(-5\theta)\).By De Moivre, this equals \((\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^{-5}\).
Section 10 of 12

Nth Roots & Roots of Unity

Roots split evenly around a circle. The angles differ by \(\frac{2\pi}{n}\).

Distinct nth rootsIf \(z=r(\cos\alpha+i\sin\alpha)\), \(r\gt0\), then its \(n\) roots are \(\displaystyle z_k=\sqrt[n]{r}\left(\cos\frac{\alpha+2k\pi}{n}+i\sin\frac{\alpha+2k\pi}{n}\right)\), \(k=0,\ldots,n-1\).
EXAMPLE 28 Cube roots
Find all distinct cube roots of \(\cos\frac{\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}\).
Here \(r=1\), \(\alpha=\frac{\pi}{3}\), \(n=3\).\(\displaystyle \theta_k=\frac{\alpha+2k\pi}{3}=\frac{\pi/3+2k\pi}{3}\), for \(k=0,1,2\).\(\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{9},\quad \mathrm{cis}\frac{7\pi}{9},\quad \mathrm{cis}\frac{13\pi}{9}\).
EXAMPLE 31 Fifth roots
Find all fifth roots of \(\sqrt3+i\).
\(\sqrt3+i=2\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{6}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{6}\right)\).\(\sqrt[5]{r}=\sqrt[5]{2}\).\(\displaystyle \theta_k=\frac{\pi/6+2k\pi}{5}=\frac{\pi}{30}+\frac{2k\pi}{5}\), \(k=0,1,2,3,4\).\(\displaystyle z_k=\sqrt[5]{2}\,\mathrm{cis}\left(\frac{\pi}{30}+\frac{2k\pi}{5}\right)\).

Roots of unity

FormulaThe \(n\) roots of \(1\) are \(\displaystyle z_k=\cos\frac{2k\pi}{n}+i\sin\frac{2k\pi}{n}\), \(k=0,\ldots,n-1\).
TRY Fourth roots of \(-16i\)
Find all four distinct fourth roots of \(-16i\).
\(-16i=16\,\mathrm{cis}\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\).Fourth root modulus: \(\sqrt[4]{16}=2\).\(\displaystyle \theta_k=\frac{-\pi/2+2k\pi}{4}=-\frac{\pi}{8}+\frac{k\pi}{2}\), \(k=0,1,2,3\).\(\displaystyle z_k=2\,\mathrm{cis}\left(-\frac{\pi}{8}+\frac{k\pi}{2}\right)\).
Section 11 of 12

De Moivre for Trig Identities & Practice

Complex numbers are also a useful tool for trigonometric identities.

Tool 1\(\cos n\theta=\operatorname{Re}(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n\), and \(\sin n\theta=\operatorname{Im}(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n\).
EXAMPLE 35 Derive \(\sin3\theta\)
Express \(\sin3\theta\) in terms of powers of \(\sin\theta\).
Let \(c=\cos\theta\), \(s=\sin\theta\). By De Moivre, \(\cos3\theta+i\sin3\theta=(c+is)^3\).
Expand: \((c+is)^3=c^3+3c^2is+3ci^2s^2+i^3s^3\).
\(=c^3-3cs^2+i(3c^2s-s^3)\).
Imaginary part: \(\sin3\theta=3c^2s-s^3\).
Use \(c^2=1-s^2\): \(\sin3\theta=3(1-s^2)s-s^3\).
\(\sin3\theta=3\sin\theta-4\sin^3\theta\).
Tool 2If \(z=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta\), then \(\displaystyle \cos\theta=\frac12\left(z+\frac1z\right)\) and \(\displaystyle \sin\theta=\frac{1}{2i}\left(z-\frac1z\right)\).
EXAMPLE 36 Express \(\cos^3\theta\)
Prove \(\displaystyle \cos^3\theta=\frac14(\cos3\theta+3\cos\theta)\).
Let \(z=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta\), so \(\cos\theta=\frac12(z+\frac1z)\).
\(\displaystyle \cos^3\theta=\left[\frac12\left(z+\frac1z\right)\right]^3\).
\(\displaystyle =\frac18\left(z^3+3z+\frac{3}{z}+\frac{1}{z^3}\right)\).
Group pairs: \(z^3+\frac1{z^3}=2\cos3\theta\), and \(z+\frac1z=2\cos\theta\).
\(\displaystyle \cos^3\theta=\frac14(\cos3\theta+3\cos\theta)\).

Mixed practice

PRACTICE 1 Regions in the complex plane
Describe the region \(|z-(1+i)|\le2\).
Let \(z=x+yi\). Then \(z-(1+i)=(x-1)+(y-1)i\).\(|z-(1+i)|\le2\) means distance from \((x,y)\) to \((1,1)\) is at most \(2\).Closed disk centred at \(1+i\), radius \(2\).
PRACTICE 2 Solve a polynomial
Solve \(z^4+1=0\).
\(z^4=-1=\mathrm{cis}\,\pi\).\(\displaystyle z_k=\mathrm{cis}\left(\frac{\pi+2k\pi}{4}\right)\), \(k=0,1,2,3\).\(\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{4},\ \mathrm{cis}\frac{3\pi}{4},\ \mathrm{cis}\frac{5\pi}{4},\ \mathrm{cis}\frac{7\pi}{4}\).
PRACTICE 3 Real expression
Show \((\sqrt3+i)^n+(\sqrt3-i)^n\) is real. Find its value when \(n=12\).
\(\sqrt3+i=2\,\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{6}\), and \(\sqrt3-i=2\,\mathrm{cis}\left(-\frac{\pi}{6}\right)\).Sum: \(2^n\left[\mathrm{cis}\frac{n\pi}{6}+\mathrm{cis}\left(-\frac{n\pi}{6}\right)\right]\).The imaginary parts cancel, so the expression is real.For \(n=12\): \(2^{12}[\mathrm{cis}(2\pi)+\mathrm{cis}(-2\pi)]=4096(1+1)\).Value \(=8192\).

Common mistakes
Argument quadrant

\(\tan^{-1}\frac{y}{x}\) gives a reference angle. The quadrant comes from the point.

All roots

An \(n\)th root question usually asks for \(n\) distinct answers.

Conjugate use

To divide rectangular forms, multiply top and bottom by the conjugate of the denominator.

Power angles

De Moivre multiplies the angle. It does not multiply sine and cosine separately.

5 minute self check
1. What is \(|3-4i|\)?
\(5\).
2. What is \(\overline{2+7i}\)?
\(2-7i\).
3. What happens to arguments when complex numbers multiply?
The arguments add, up to full turns.
4. How many fifth roots does a nonzero complex number have?
Five, equally spaced by \(\frac{2\pi}{5}\).
5. What region is \(|z-(1+i)|\le2\)?
A closed disk centred at \(1+i\), radius \(2\).
